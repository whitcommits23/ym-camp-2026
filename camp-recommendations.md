# YM Camp 2026 — Leader Recommendations
**Pioneer Trail Camp | June 8–12 | 57 youth, 5 wards**

---

**Bottom line:** The schedule and activities are solid. The four gaps worth closing before June 8 are all logistical — not spiritual. If you fix these, your leaders spend the week leading instead of managing chaos.

---

## 1. Targeted wristbands for safety-critical boys only

Do this, but narrow the scope. A wristband on every boy with a health note (you'd have 30+) turns a safety tool into background noise. Leaders stop reading them. The point of a wristband is that a leader who *doesn't know the kid* can act correctly in the moment.

Limit it to 5 boys where the consequence of a stranger not knowing is serious:

- **Red** — Water restriction. No swimming without a life vest. (Kenson Dickey)
- **Orange** — Weak swimmer. Needs close monitoring in water. (Matthew Tracy)
- **Blue** — Activity restriction. ACL recovery. Confirm what's cleared. (Will Gundersen)
- **Yellow** — Medical alert. Type 1 Diabetes. Know his signs. (Hunter Aardema)
- **Yellow** — Medical alert. Severe food allergy. (Ryker Gerstenslager — pistachios/cashews)

Everyone else stays off the wristband system. Their health info lives on the leader card (see #2). The wristband is only for "someone who has never met this kid needs to make the right call in the next 30 seconds."

Silicone wristbands are cheap. Get them in the right colors, write the boy's name in Sharpie, put them on Monday at check-in.

## 2. Per-adult medical reference card for off-site days

Tuesday (canoeing), Wednesday (rock climbing), and Thursday (WhirlyBall) all involve buses and venues where your boys mix across wards. The Youngstown bishop knows Kenson. The adult counting heads at Camp Hi Canoes probably doesn't.

Make a laminated half-sheet for each adult leader listing every youth they're accountable for on that day's excursion. It doesn't need to be a full medical form — just: name, one-line flag, and action. For example:

> Kenson Dickey — Youngstown — NO SWIMMING without life vest. May go non-verbal if overwhelmed. Contact Dave [leader] if he shuts down.

> Kai Field — Youngstown — Asthma. Give inhaler 10 min before activity. If anxious, don't push.

> Hunter Aardema — Hiram — Type 1 Diabetes. If he goes pale, confused, or shaky: juice box immediately, then get an adult leader.

The reason this matters specifically at canoeing: water is the highest-stakes activity on the schedule, it's the first full day, and the boys haven't settled in yet. That's when confusion about who knows what gets someone hurt.

Build this card from the roster before camp. One card per adult, laminated, in their pocket for every off-site day.

## 3. Intentional cross-ward teams for Wednesday and Thursday activities

The theme is "Walk with Me." The gravitational pull of this group is Tallmadge with Tallmadge, Rootstown with Rootstown. An 18-year-old Priest from Youngstown and an 11-year-old Deacon from Hiram will not find each other on their own.

Research on cross-age peer mentoring is consistent: older boys who are placed in a mentoring role with younger ones grow in responsibility, and younger boys gain confidence faster. Both benefit more than if they stay in peer-only groups.

Wednesday's scavenger hunt and Thursday's Survivor Trail Challenge are both perfect vehicles. Assign teams intentionally: 4-5 boys, one from each quorum (Priest leads, Teacher supports, Deacon participates), and mix the wards. Thursday's theme is literally "We Walk with Each Other." Use it.

The two hours of mixed-age team competition will do more for inter-ward relationships than any fireside. It also gives your older Priests a real role — not just "the older guys" but the person a 12-year-old is actually looking to.

Brief your Priest quorum on Monday night: this week, you're not just here for you. Point to a Deacon and say "watch out for him."

## 4. Quiet reset protocol for high-sensory boys

You have at least 4 boys whose notes flag sensory overload, anxiety, or autism: Kenson Dickey (may go non-verbal when scared), Kai Field (autism + ADHD, may get anxious/feel sick to his stomach), Friedrich Mix (high anxiety, music is a calming tool), and Leo Sims (noise sensitivity, especially chewing — may need to separate at meals). Camp is loud and crowded for 5 days straight.

These boys can have great weeks. But only if leaders know what to do *before* it escalates, not after.

Three specific things to put in place before arrival:

**Designate a reset spot.** One quiet corner of the mess hall, or a bench away from the main activity area. Every leader knows where it is. No explanation needed when a boy goes there — it's just a spot to breathe.

**Brief the right leaders, not all leaders.** Each of these boys has a ward leader who knows him. That leader needs a 60-second private brief before camp: "Here's what you'll see, here's what works, here's what makes it worse." For Kenson: going non-verbal isn't panic — don't push him to talk. For Friedrich: a pair of earbuds and a few minutes of music actually works. For Leo: seat him at the end of a table at meals, not the middle.

**Have a plan for meals specifically.** The chewing noise sensitivity is a narrow flag but it's real. Mess halls are the worst environment for Leo Sims. Just seat him at the end, near a wall, away from concentrated eating noise. Takes 10 seconds to plan and prevents a daily rough spot.

None of this is coddling. The boy who has a bad Tuesday because nobody knew what he needed is the one who never comes back. A 30-minute prep conversation with 4 ward leaders is the investment.
